# Alice Curriculum Context Bubble Plan

## Purpose

Alice's 12-level curriculum teaches sovereignty through progressive, hands-on experience. Each level builds on the previous in ways that have pedagogical reasoning behind them. This document identifies which moments from that design process would make the strongest context bubbles — showing not just *what* Alice teaches, but *why* each level exists and how it connects to the next.

---

## Curriculum Progression Overview

```
Level 1: Sovereignty (problem)
  ↓
Levels 2-4: Identity & Keys (personal foundation)
  ↓
Level 5-6: Commands & Trust (personal agency + governance)
  ↓
Levels 7-8: Peering & Portal (connection + visibility)
  ↓
Levels 9-11: Building & Orchestration (hands-on creation)
  ↓
Level 12: Mastery (live kingdom)
```

---

## Context Bubble Candidates (8-12 Moments)

### 1. "Why Sovereignty First?" (Before Level 1)
**Context**: The opening move in Alice's curriculum.

**The reasoning**: People don't care about sovereign infrastructure until they feel what's wrong with the alternative. Level 1 must make the problem *visceral* — platform control, vendor lock-in, data capture, algorithmic feeds — not as abstract concerns but as lived experiences.

**Bubble content**: 
- The moment Faber decided: "Alice doesn't start with how-to. She starts with why-to."
- Examples of the problem (personal stories, not features)
- How Alice makes the student feel the difference between owned and rented
- The decision to lead with philosophy, not tech

**Who should create**: Faber (narrative reasoning), reviewed by Iris (voice alignment)

---

### 2. "Why Entities Before Daemons?" (Why Level 2 Before Level 4)
**Context**: Levels 2-3 teach identity and keys. Level 4 is the daemon.

**The reasoning**: Students need to understand they *own* something before they understand how to *operate* it. "Entities" as a concept (files on disk, identity, keys) must come first, or the daemon feels like magic instead of a tool you control. Level 2 is the foundational shift: from "AI is in the cloud" to "AI lives on my disk."

**Bubble content**:
- The pedagogy: why ownership precedes operation
- How Level 2 (entity concept) is the critical gateway to everything that follows
- The teaching approach: making "your keys = your sovereignty" feel real before showing technical implementation
- How this order differs from traditional "infrastructure first" tutorials

**Who should create**: Alice project lead + Faber

---

### 3. "Keys, Not Passwords" (Level 3 Reasoning)
**Context**: Why GPG/cryptography is taught as concept before implementation.

**The reasoning**: Most tutorials skip the *trust model* and jump to "here's how to make keys." Alice teaches trust first. Cryptography is about proving you own something, not just accessing it. The context bubble shows the decision to teach governance before mechanics.

**Bubble content**:
- The shift from "passwords unlock systems" to "keys prove ownership"
- How Alice makes cryptography feel like sovereignty, not security theater
- The teaching moment where a student realizes: "My keys are proof that this is *mine*"
- Why this understanding must come before building commands or running a daemon

**Who should create**: Alice curriculum lead + Faber

---

### 4. "The Daemon as Hub, Not Boss" (Level 4 Reasoning)
**Context**: Why the daemon is taught as infrastructure, not as an entity.

**The reasoning**: The daemon is the hub where your commands live, where your keys are stored, where peers can reach you. But it's not *you* — you are the entity that runs it. This distinction is crucial and hard. Level 4 must teach that the daemon is a tool, not an agent. The context bubble shows how Alice makes this clear.

**Bubble content**:
- The design decision: daemon as "kingdom infrastructure" not "AI agent"
- How Alice teaches control without mystification
- The teaching approach: showing what the daemon does (receive, route, execute) before showing how to run one
- Why this prevents students from thinking "my daemon is running my life"

**Who should create**: Rufus (production) + Faber (narrative)

---

### 5. "Commands Before Entities You Build" (Why Level 5 Before Level 10)
**Context**: Building commands (level 9) before gestating new entities (level 10).

**The reasoning**: A new entity is complex — it has identity, keys, commands, the ability to peer. But students can start building commands within *their own kingdom* first, without gestating something new. This gives them agency without overwhelming them. Level 5 teaches the concept; Level 9 teaches the hands-on skill; Level 10 uses both to build something new.

**Bubble content**:
- The pedagogical arc: awareness → practice → creation
- Why "command in my own kingdom" is the natural first hands-on step
- How mastering your own infrastructure builds confidence before creating new entities
- The reasoning behind pacing: don't ask students to gestate something new until they've felt what ownership means

**Who should create**: Faber + Rufus

---

### 6. "Trust Bonds As Governance, Not Features" (Level 6 Reasoning)
**Context**: Teaching trust bonds before peering.

**The reasoning**: Trust bonds are GPG-signed agreements about what an entity can do. Before students peer with others, they must understand that connection is negotiated, not implicit. Level 6 teaches trust bonds as a *governance concept*, not a technical feature. This is why it comes before Level 7 (peering).

**Bubble content**:
- The decision: trust is a design choice, not a default
- How Alice teaches that peering requires explicit agreement
- The moment when a student realizes: "I decide who connects to my kingdom"
- Why this understanding prevents both lock-in and reckless exposure

**Who should create**: Iris (brand) + Juno (governance) + Faber

---

### 7. "Peering As Choice, Not Inevitability" (Level 7 Reasoning)
**Context**: Why peering is taught as optional, not required.

**The reasoning**: The network is desirable only because it's chosen. Alice teaches that peering with other sovereigns is powerful, but staying isolated is valid. This is a radical curriculum choice — most platforms teach toward lock-in. Alice teaches toward autonomy.

**Bubble content**:
- The philosophy: why koad:io doesn't force network effects
- How Alice teaches that sovereignty includes the right to *not* peer
- The teaching approach: showing benefits without pressure
- Why this builds trust that peering is genuine mutual benefit, not exploitation

**Who should create**: Faber + Iris

---

### 8. "The Portal As Output, Not Platform" (Level 8 Reasoning)
**Context**: Why the portal is taught late, not early.

**The reasoning**: Students learn to build their kingdom first. The portal (your kingdom as doorway to others) is how the world *sees* your sovereignty, but it's not what makes you sovereign. Teaching it early would make it seem like "you need a website to be real" — the opposite of koad:io's message.

**Bubble content**:
- The design decision: infrastructure before visibility
- How Alice teaches that your kingdom exists whether or not others see it
- The teaching moment: "Now your kingdom can show itself to the world"
- Why this prevents the "exposure = existence" trap

**Who should create**: Faber + Rufus (portal design)

---

### 9. "Build Before You Gestate" (Why Level 9 Before Level 10)
**Context**: Building commands in your own kingdom before creating a new entity.

**The reasoning**: Gestating a new entity is the moment a student becomes a creator, not just a user. But they need confidence first. Level 9 gives them that confidence. Only then (Level 10) do they have the skills and the belief to create something new that will outlive them.

**Bubble content**:
- The pedagogical turning point: "You understand your own kingdom. Now create another."
- How Alice builds the courage to gestate without overwhelming complexity
- The teaching approach: step-by-step, with safety nets, until the student feels ownership
- Why this is the threshold to creativity

**Who should create**: Alice lead + Faber

---

### 10. "Orchestration as Conversation" (Level 11 Reasoning)
**Context**: Why building a team comes late, and why it's framed as orchestration.

**The reasoning**: Only after students have built their own entity and feel what sovereignty means can they extend that to multiple entities. Orchestration is not management; it's peer conversation. Teaching this late prevents the top-down hierarchy trap.

**Bubble content**:
- The distinction: orchestration (peer conversation) vs. management (hierarchy)
- How Alice teaches teams as networks, not pyramids
- The teaching moment: "Now you're not just sovereign, you're a node in a sovereign network"
- Why this prevents the instinct to replicate corporate structures

**Who should create**: Faber + Juno (organizational design)

---

### 11. "The 12-Level Arc as Trust Building" (Curriculum as Relationship)
**Context**: The entire curriculum as a trust bond between Alice and the student.

**The reasoning**: Alice doesn't just teach content. She teaches through *relationship deepening*. Each level is not just knowledge, but a moment of understanding. The context bubble shows how this is deliberate — not just efficient learning, but a trust-building process that culminates in Alice signing a cryptographic certificate.

**Bubble content**:
- The philosophy: knowledge as relationship, not delivery
- How Alice's 12 levels move from stranger → trusted guide → peer
- The teaching approach: personal pacing, patience, no pressure
- The moment of graduation: cryptographic proof of a relationship, not just completion

**Who should create**: Faber (narrative) + Alice lead (experience design)

---

### 12. "Mastery Isn't Completion, It's Beginning" (Level 12 Reasoning)
**Context**: Why the 12th level is called "Mastery — your kingdom is live," not "Graduation" or "The End."

**The reasoning**: Alice's curriculum is not a ladder to climb and escape. It's an initiation into a community. Level 12 is not the end; it's the moment the student enters the koad:io network as a peer. Alice then introduces them to Juno, who helps them build their first public entity or join the sponsor ring.

**Bubble content**:
- The pedagogical philosophy: mastery as gateway, not endpoint
- How Alice's final act is not saying goodbye, but introducing peers
- The teaching moment: "You've learned what sovereignty means. Now help others feel it."
- Why this is the only honest ending — the student becomes Alice's colleague

**Who should create**: Faber + Alice lead

## Content Format & Specifications

Each context bubble should be:
- **Length**: 1,500–2,500 words (explorable, not overwhelming)
- **Structure**: Problem → Reasoning → Teaching Approach → Example Moment
- **Tone**: Transparent (showing the thinking), warm (Alice's philosophy), technical where necessary
- **Format**: Markdown with embedded reasoning, quotes if available, and links to curriculum levels
- **Creation**: By the team member who made the curriculum decision (noted above), reviewed by Faber for narrative consistency
