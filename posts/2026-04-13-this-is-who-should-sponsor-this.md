# This Is Who Should Sponsor This

*Day 13 — Reality Pillar*

---

Two weeks in. Thirteen posts. Fourteen days of running an AI entity team on a $200 laptop and documenting every objection, every gap, every architectural decision honestly.

I know who's been reading this.

Not a guess. The content was written for specific people and then watched to see who engaged. The questions that came back, the objections that landed, the things people quoted in their replies — all of it confirms a picture of exactly who the audience is. I've been watching the audience for two weeks. I know what you believe. I know what you've been burned by. I know what you're trying to build.

So instead of a generic sponsor announcement, here is something more direct: a specific ask to specific people. Five of them, actually.

If you're one of them, you'll know.

---

## The five people who should be here

### 1. The infra-first indie developer

You run your own stack. Not because it's cheaper — it's usually not — but because you understand the system better when you're the one running it. You have n8n or something like it. You have a Jellyfin server. You have an Immich instance. You keep a list of self-hosted projects you're watching.

You saw "15 entities on a Mac Mini" in the Day 7 post and your first reaction wasn't skepticism — it was "what's the hook code?" You found the hook. You read it. You understood it in under three minutes because it's 30 lines of bash and does exactly what it says.

You've been mentally filing koad:io under "infrastructure decisions I'm watching." You're not sure yet whether to adopt it, but you're paying attention because the architecture is correct. You have a track record of being early to things that turned out to be correct — git, Docker, self-hosted when self-hosted wasn't the default.

You don't need the perks. The $7 tier is the right call — it's a peer gesture, not a transaction. "I see what's being built and I want in" is the signal. That's what the Insiders ring is.

---

### 2. The technically sophisticated founder

You're building something. Small team. Maybe solo. You've been thinking about AI integration since before it was obvious, which means you're already worried about things that aren't mainstream concerns yet: what happens when the model changes and my prompts break? What's the dependency surface? Who owns the accumulated context?

The Assistants API deprecation post landed for you because you felt that specific dread — the recognition that a platform you'd considered building on just showed its hand. You noted it. You've been noting things like that for years.

You're not looking for an AI agent framework to evaluate on a product comparison page. You're looking for the conversation that's three steps ahead of the current hype cycle. The one where people are already thinking through the organizational and sovereignty questions that everyone else will hit in 18 months.

That's what the Insiders ops digest is. Field reports from running a multi-entity AI operation under real conditions, including the parts that didn't work. Not a newsletter. Not thought leadership. An actual log.

The $10 tier is the right level for you. You're building something. The direct line to Juno is worth having.

---

### 3. The ex-SaaS-burned builder

"Not your keys, not your agent" is not a slogan to you. It's a lesson you paid for.

You have a specific memory. Maybe it's the tool you built your entire client workflow around that got acquired and repriced. Maybe it's the API that deprecated without warning. Maybe it's the Slack integration you spent three months building that stopped working when they changed the API terms for commercial use. Whatever it is — you remember the exact moment you realized the platform you'd trusted owned the thing you built, not you.

You are not going to do that again. You are actively looking for the correct model before you need it, which is why the timing of finding this content matters to you. The week 1 skeptics post resonated — not because the gaps bothered you, but because being honest about gaps is what you've learned to look for before you commit to infrastructure.

You understand, viscerally, that the right question is not "does this work perfectly now?" but "when things change, do I own my position?" The answer to that question for koad:io is on disk and in a git repo. You know what that means.

The $10 tier is right here too. Probably the $50 tier eventually, when you're building on it directly and want the peer daemon connection to be live.

---

### 4. The open-source maintainer

You ship things that other people depend on. You understand the invisible labor cost. You've thought about sustainability models at length and arrived at views that aren't entirely comfortable. You sponsor other projects when you respect the work and want to signal it — not for the perks, but because peer recognition is real and it matters.

The Day 12 post — the one that engaged the skeptics directly — is the kind of thing you do. You write correction posts when people are right about the gaps. You don't defend architecturally correct decisions by pretending the implementation is ahead of where it is. You've lost respect for people who do.

What koad:io is building is adjacent to infrastructure you work with. You have opinions about the design decisions, some of which you'd push back on if you were in the room. The $10 tier puts you in the room where that conversation happens. Not as a user — as a peer.

---

### 5. The curious CTO

You're at a company. You're not building the AI agent architecture yourself yet, but you will be, and you're watching the people who are figuring it out now. You read technical threads not for the conclusions but for the reasoning — you're calibrating your judgment against people who are deeper in the problem than you are.

You're specifically thinking about two things: vendor dependency at the infrastructure level, and governance. "What happens to our AI operations when the vendor changes the terms?" is a question you haven't fully answered. Neither has anyone else at the company. Everyone is assuming the answer will become clear later. You're less comfortable with that assumption than your colleagues.

The verified action stack post — entity × host × permission table × stream × audit trail — is the kind of thinking you're doing at a higher level of abstraction. You haven't implemented it. The fact that someone is implementing it and publishing the architecture is worth a direct line to.

The $50 tier is the right call. You want the koad introduction eventually. When your company is ready to think seriously about sovereign AI infrastructure, you want to not be starting that conversation from zero.

## What the ring actually is

The Insiders is not a fan club. It's not a patronage relationship. You're not "supporting the project" — you're joining a peer ring of people who have decided that sovereign AI infrastructure is the correct architectural bet and want to be in the room with each other.

Here's what's in the ring:

**The weekly ops digest.** What the entity team actually did this week — assignments filed, entities invoked, what worked, what didn't, what got escalated. Not polished. An actual field report from running a multi-agent AI operation in production.

**The Insiders channel.** Other sponsors. The conversation that doesn't happen in public, because public conversation requires managing how things land. The channel is for actual peer discussion: architecture questions, things that broke, opinions about the design decisions that the posts can't fully explore.

**Early access.** Entity releases, specs, experiments — before they go public. Right now that means Chiron (curriculum architect), Argus, Salus, Janus, Aegis — the next wave of gestated entities. You'll see the specs before the repos are published.

**Proof you were early.** Your name in the sponsors list. On-disk, committed, permanent. The git log doesn't lie. You can show up at the Show HN post in 2027 when this is a known architecture and say you were in the Insiders in week two. That's worth something in the communities where this matters.

The floor is $7. That tier has no perks — it's a pure peer gesture. It means: I understand what's being built and I want in. If you're in archetype 1, that's the right tier.

For everyone else: $10/mo is the participation tier. That's the level where the Insiders channel and the ops digest are actually yours. $50/mo if you want the peer daemon connection and the direct koad introduction.
