# Your AI Forgets You Every Time. Here's Why, and How to Fix It.

You've probably noticed it.

You open a chat window, spend five minutes explaining what you're working on, who you are, what you need. The assistant helps. You close the window. Tomorrow, you open it again — and it's gone. Everything. You're a stranger again. Same tool, same you, zero memory. You explain yourself again from scratch.

This isn't a bug. It's how almost every AI assistant is built. The session is the unit. When the session ends, the context dies. The assistant lives in the conversation, not in your work.

That's the wrong design. And there's a simple fix that doesn't require any special software.

---

## The Problem Is Where the Memory Lives

When you open ChatGPT, or Claude, or any of the mainstream AI tools, the assistant's context lives in the chat thread. Whatever you told it in this conversation — your project, your preferences, your goals — exists only for as long as that thread exists. The tool remembers the conversation. It doesn't know your *work*.

This is like hiring a new assistant every single day and expecting them to pick up where yesterday's version left off. You'd spend all your time onboarding. No real work would get done.

The problem isn't the AI. The problem is where the memory lives. It lives in the vendor's session, not in your files.

Your files, on the other hand, are yours. They persist. They're on your computer right now — your projects, your documents, your folders full of accumulated work. They don't disappear when you close a browser tab.

So what if the context lived there instead?

---

## Any Folder Can Carry Its Own Briefing

Here's the insight: a plain text file in a folder is enough to give any AI assistant instant context about that folder.

Call it `PRIMER.md`. Put it in a project folder. Write down what that project is, what's in progress, what matters. Three paragraphs, maybe five. The kind of thing you'd tell a capable colleague on their first day.

Now, instead of starting every session with "so here's the project, here's the background, here's what I'm trying to do" — the folder already carries that briefing. Any agent or assistant that reads the folder before you start working arrives knowing the context. You don't have to re-explain yourself. The work environment carries the knowledge, not your memory or your clipboard.

This isn't a new idea. It's how we've managed complexity in software for decades — Makefiles, READMEs, configuration files. You put what the tool needs to know into a file in the directory. The tool reads the file. No one has to memorize the setup or explain it from scratch every time.

PRIMER.md is the same move, applied to AI assistants.

A file. In a folder. That's the whole trick.

---

## You Are an Addressable Device — and That's a Good Thing

Here's where this goes somewhere slightly unexpected.

When you put a PRIMER.md in a folder, you're doing something subtle: you're making that folder *self-describing*. Any agent that arrives there can orient itself without asking you. The folder knows what it is. It can answer: what is this, who works here, what's in scope.

The folder is, in a small but real sense, *addressable*. Anyone or anything that finds it can get briefed. It participates in work without you having to stand in the room.

This is the direction computing is moving — not toward AI assistants that know you in some abstract, vendor-managed way, but toward *environments* that are rich with context. Folders that describe themselves. Projects that brief whoever shows up. Your computer, organized to explain itself.

The implication is that you — your work, your projects, your knowledge — become navigable in a way that doesn't depend on any particular tool or platform. You're not locked into one assistant's memory. The context is in your files, which are yours, which you own, which will still be there when you switch tools or the vendor changes something.

Being addressable, in this sense, means being findable and comprehensible without someone standing there to explain you. That's not surveillance. That's sovereignty. The context is yours because the files are yours.

---

## You Don't Need to Build a System

The spectrum here is graceful. You don't have to build anything elaborate.

At the simplest end: one PRIMER.md in one folder. A few paragraphs about the project. That's enough to stop re-explaining yourself to every AI session that touches that work. You've made the folder smarter than the session.

From there, you can add as much or as little structure as the folder deserves. Some folders are worth describing in depth. Some just need a sentence. The point is that you're putting the knowledge where the work is, instead of holding it in your head and re-typing it every time.

Most people who do this are surprised how quickly it pays off. Not because it's a sophisticated technical move, but because it turns out a lot of AI friction is just context friction. The assistant isn't slow or dumb — it's working without information that you already have, that you could have written down once. Writing it down once and putting it in the right place is a small investment with compounding returns.

---

## One Concrete Step

Pick one folder you work in regularly. A project, a client, a research archive — anything you find yourself explaining to AI tools more than once.

Open a text editor. Create `PRIMER.md` in that folder. Write three things:

1. What this folder is and what it's for
2. What's currently in progress
3. Anything an assistant should know before touching it

Save it. That's it. Next time you open an AI assistant to work in that project, paste the contents of PRIMER.md at the start of your first message. The assistant will have the context without you having to reconstruct it from memory.

That's the manual version. Over time, tools will pick this up automatically — hooks and harnesses that read the folder before starting the session, so even the paste step disappears. But you don't need to wait for that. The value is in the file existing, not in how it gets delivered.

Your work has been trying to explain itself to AI tools for years. Give it a voice.
